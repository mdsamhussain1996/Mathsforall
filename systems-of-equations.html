Introduction & Overview

The foundation of simultaneous constraints

A system of linear equations is a set of two or more linear equations in the same variables. In most cases, we work with two equations and two variables (usually \(x\) and \(y\)). A solution to such a system is an ordered pair \((x, y)\) that satisfies every equation in the system simultaneously. Geometrically, this solution corresponds to the point where the lines represented by the equations intersect.

There are three possible outcomes when solving a system: the lines intersect at exactly one point, the lines are parallel (no solution), or the lines are identical (infinitely many solutions). Understanding these systems is crucial for solving real-life problems like break-even analysis, where you find the point where costs and revenue are equal, or resource allocation problems in engineering and economics.

📖 Core Vocabulary

  • System of linear equations: A set of two or more linear equations in the same variables.
  • Solution of a system: An ordered pair that is a solution of each equation in the system.
  • Consistent system: A system that has at least one solution.
  • Inconsistent system: A system that has no solution.
  • Dependent system: A consistent system with infinitely many solutions.

5.1 Solving Systems by Graphing

Visualizing the intersection of constraints

⚙️ Step-by-Step Method

1 Graph each equation in the same coordinate plane.
2 Estimate the point of intersection.
3 Check the point by substituting into both original equations.

Worked Example 1: Basic Graphing Solution

Solve the system:
\(y = -2x + 5\) (Equation 1)
\(y = 4x - 1\) (Equation 2)

Step 1: Graph both lines. Equation 1 has y-intercept 5 and slope -2. Equation 2 has y-intercept -1 and slope 4.

Step 2: The lines appear to intersect at (1, 3).

Step 3: Check:
Eq 1: \(3 = -2(1) + 5 \rightarrow 3 = 3\) ✓
Eq 2: \(3 = 4(1) - 1 \rightarrow 3 = 3\) ✓

Worked Example 2: Roofing Contractor (Real-Life)

A roofing contractor buys 30 bundles of shingles and 4 rolls of roofing paper for $1040. In a second purchase, the contractor buys 8 bundles of shingles for $256. Find the price of each.

System:
\(30x + 4y = 1040\)
\(8x = 256\)

From the second equation, \(x = 256/8 = 32\). Substituting into the first: \(30(32) + 4y = 1040 \rightarrow 960 + 4y = 1040 \rightarrow 4y = 80 \rightarrow y = 20\).

Interpretation: Price per bundle = $32, Price per roll = $20.

Practice Problems (5.1)

1. Check if (2, 6) is a solution for: \(x + y = 8\) and \(3x - y = 0\).

Solution: \(2+6=8\) (Yes) and \(3(2)-6=0\) (Yes). It is a solution.

2. Solve by graphing: \(y = -x + 7\) and \(y = x + 1\).

Solution: Intersection at (3, 4).

5.2 Solving Systems by Substitution

Algebraic precision through variable isolation

⚙️ Step-by-Step Method

1 Solve one equation for one variable.
2 Substitute that expression into the other equation and solve.
3 Substitute the found value back to get the second variable.
4 Check in BOTH original equations.

Worked Example 1: Direct Substitution

Solve:
\(y = -2x - 9\) (Eq 1)
\(6x - 5y = -19\) (Eq 2)

Step 1: Eq 1 is already solved for \(y\).

Step 2: Substitute \((-2x - 9)\) for \(y\) in Eq 2:
\(6x - 5(-2x - 9) = -19\)
\(6x + 10x + 45 = -19\)
\(16x = -64 \rightarrow x = -4\)

Step 3: \(y = -2(-4) - 9 = 8 - 9 = -1\).

Solution: (-4, -1).

💡
Pro Tip: Choose the equation where a variable already has a coefficient of 1 or -1. This minimizes fractions and makes the algebra much cleaner!
Practice Problems (5.2)

Solve using substitution: \(x + y = 180\) and \(x = 2y\).

Solution: \(2y + y = 180 \rightarrow 3y = 180 \rightarrow y = 60, x = 120\). Solution: (120, 60).

5.3 Solving Systems by Elimination

Removing variables through addition and subtraction

⚙️ Step-by-Step Method

1 Multiply one or both equations by a constant so coefficients of one variable are opposites.
2 Add the equations to eliminate that variable.
3 Solve the resulting single-variable equation.
4 Substitute back to find the other variable.

Worked Example 2: Multiply One Equation

Solve:
\(-10x + 3y = 1\)
\(-5x - 6y = 23\)

Step 1: Multiply Equation 2 by -2 to get \(10x\).
\(-2(-5x - 6y) = -2(23) \rightarrow 10x + 12y = -46\)

Step 2: Add to Equation 1:
\((-10x + 3y) + (10x + 12y) = 1 + (-46)\)
\(15y = -45 \rightarrow y = -3\)

Step 3: \(-10x + 3(-3) = 1 \rightarrow -10x - 9 = 1 \rightarrow -10x = 10 \rightarrow x = -1\).

Solution: (-1, -3).

Choosing the Best Method

Method When to Use
Graphing To estimate solutions or visualize relationships.
Substitution When a variable in either equation has a coefficient of 1 or -1.
Elimination When like terms have the same or opposite coefficients.
Elimination (Mult) When no variable can be directly eliminated.

5.4 Special Systems

No solution vs. Infinitely many solutions

Case 1: No Solution

Lines are parallel. They have the same slope but different y-intercepts. Algebraic solving leads to a contradiction (e.g., \(0 = 5\)).

Case 2: Infinitely Many Solutions

Lines are identical. They have the same slope and same y-intercept. Algebraic solving leads to an identity (e.g., \(0 = 0\)).

Worked Example 1: No Solution

Solve: \(y = 2x + 1\) and \(y = 2x - 5\)

By substitution: \(2x + 1 = 2x - 5 \rightarrow 1 = -5\). This is false. No solution.

5.5 Solving Equations by Graphing

Using systems to solve single-variable equations

To solve \(ax + b = cx + d\) graphically:

  1. Write \(y = ax + b\) (Eq 1)
  2. Write \(y = cx + d\) (Eq 2)
  3. The x-coordinate of the intersection point is the solution.

5.6 & 5.7 Linear Inequalities

Graphing regions and overlapping solutions

Graphing Steps
  1. Graph boundary line (Dashed for \(<\), \(>\); Solid for \(\leq\), \(\geq\)).
  2. Test a point (usually \((0,0)\)).
  3. Shade the side that makes the inequality true.
Systems of Inequalities

The solution is the overlapping region where all individual inequality shaded areas intersect.

Worked Example 4: Fruit Salad Budget

At most $10 on grapes ($2.50/lb) and apples ($1/lb).
Inequality: \(2.5x + y \leq 10\)

Test (0,0): \(2.5(0) + 0 \leq 10 \rightarrow 0 \leq 10\) ✓. Shade toward the origin. Solutions must be in Quadrant I (positive weights).

From Equations to Matrices: Why We Need a New Approach

Scaling up to higher dimensions

The graphical and substitution/elimination methods we've covered work beautifully for \(2 \times 2\) systems. However, as we move into the real world—modeling complex systems like global logistics, weather patterns, or neural networks—we often encounter systems with hundreds or even thousands of variables. Solving a system of 10 equations with 10 unknowns algebraically would be extremely tedious and prone to human error.

This is where Matrices come in. By representing a system compactly as a matrix, we can organize the elimination process systematically. The Augmented Matrix is a natural extension of the elimination method you've already learned. Gaussian Elimination is essentially the same process of "canceling out" variables, but handled with the structural rigor of matrix algebra.

Definition: Augmented Matrix

Given the system:

\[\begin{cases} a_1x + b_1y + c_1z = d_1 \\ a_2x + b_2y + c_2z = d_2 \\ a_3x + b_3y + c_3z = d_3 \end{cases}\]

The augmented matrix is represented as:

\[\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} a_1 & b_1 & c_1 & d_1 \\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 & d_2 \\ a_3 & b_3 & c_3 & d_3 \end{array} \right]\]

The vertical bar separates the coefficient matrix (\(A\)) from the constants vector (\(b\)). We write this compactly as \([A | b]\).

📖 Core Matrix Vocabulary

  • Matrix: A rectangular array of numbers.
  • Entry: An individual number inside a matrix.
  • Dimensions: \(m \times n\) (rows by columns).
  • Square Matrix: A matrix where \(m = n\).
  • Leading Entry: The leftmost nonzero entry in a row.
  • Zero Row: A row consisting entirely of zeros.
📊

5.8 Row Echelon Form (REF)

The standard "staircase" structure for matrix reduction

Formal Definition: Row Echelon Form

A matrix is in Row Echelon Form (REF) if and only if:

  1. All zero rows are at the bottom of the matrix.
  2. The leading entry (pivot) of each nonzero row is strictly to the right of the leading entry of the row above it.
  3. All entries below each leading entry are zero.

Note: In REF, leading entries can be any nonzero value. If they are all 1, it is a step closer to RREF.

REF Visual Pattern
\[ \begin{bmatrix} \color{#3b82f6}{\text{★}} & * & * & * & * \\ 0 & \color{#3b82f6}{\text{★}} & * & * & * \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \color{#3b82f6}{\text{★}} & * \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

\(\text{★} = \text{Pivot (nonzero)}\), \(* = \text{Any value}\)

Elementary Row Operations

These are the only three allowed operations that keep the solution set unchanged:

OperationNotationMeaning
Row Swap\(R_i \leftrightarrow R_j\)Interchange two rows
Row Scaling\(kR_i \rightarrow R_i\)Multiply a row by a nonzero constant \(k\)
Row Replacement\(R_i + kR_j \rightarrow R_i\)Replace a row by sum of itself and a multiple of another
Pivot vs Free Variables

Pivot Variables: Columns containing a leading entry. These are determined by back-substitution.

Free Variables: Columns with no leading entry. These generate infinitely many solutions.

Rank of a Matrix

The rank of matrix \(A\) is the number of nonzero rows in its REF. It indicates the number of independent constraints in the system.

Worked Example: 3×3 System (Unique Solution)

Solve using REF and back-substitution:
\(x + 2y - z = 3\) | \(2x + 7y + z = 16\) | \(x + 3y + 2z = 10\)

AUGMENTED MATRIX \[\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & -1 & 3 \\ 2 & 7 & 1 & 16 \\ 1 & 3 & 2 & 10 \end{array} \right]\]
R₂ → R₂ - 2R₁ | R₃ → R₃ - R₁ \[\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 3 & 3 & 10 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 & 7 \end{array} \right]\]
R₃ → R₃ - (1/3)R₂ \[\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 3 & 3 & 10 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 11/3 \end{array} \right] \text{ (REF Achieved)}\]

Back-Substitution:

  1. From Row 3: \(2z = 11/3 \rightarrow z = 11/6\)
  2. From Row 2: \(3y + 3(11/6) = 10 \rightarrow 3y = 9/2 \rightarrow y = 3/2\)
  3. From Row 1: \(x + 2(3/2) - 11/6 = 3 \rightarrow x = 11/6\)

Solution: \((11/6, 3/2, 11/6)\).

Practice Problems (5.8)

1. Is this matrix in REF? \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

Solution: No. Zero row is not at the bottom.

2. Find the rank of: \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 5 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\)

Solution: Rank = 2 (two nonzero rows).

5.9 Reduced Row Echelon Form (RREF)

The unique, canonical form for direct solution reading

Formal Definition: RREF

A matrix is in Reduced Row Echelon Form (RREF) if it satisfies all REF conditions PLUS:

  1. Each leading entry (pivot) is exactly 1.
  2. Each leading 1 is the only nonzero entry in its column (zeros above and below).
✨
The Uniqueness Theorem: Unlike REF, every matrix has a unique RREF. No matter which row operations you use, everyone will arrive at the exact same RREF for a given matrix.

Worked Example: 2×2 System to RREF

Solve: \(3x + y = 7\) and \(x - 2y = 4\)

R₁ ↔ R₂ (Swap for easy pivot) \[\left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & -2 & 4 \\ 3 & 1 & 7 \end{array} \right]\]
R₂ → R₂ - 3R₁ \[\left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & -2 & 4 \\ 0 & 7 & -5 \end{array} \right]\]
(1/7)R₂ → R₂ (Normalization) \[\left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & -2 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & -5/7 \end{array} \right] \text{ (REF)}\]
R₁ → R₁ + 2R₂ (Backward Elimination) \[\left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 0 & 18/7 \\ 0 & 1 & -5/7 \end{array} \right] \text{ (RREF)}\]

Read directly: \(x = 18/7, y = -5/7\). No back-substitution required!

Practice Problems (5.9)

1. Convert REF \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & | & 5 \\ 0 & 1 & | & 2 \end{bmatrix}\) to RREF.

Solution: \(R_1 \to R_1 - 2R_2 \implies \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & | & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & | & 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

5.10 Gaussian and Gauss-Jordan Elimination

The computational algorithms behind the forms

Gaussian Elimination

Process: Reduce to REF + Back-Substitution.

Phase 1: Forward Elimination (clear below pivots).

Phase 2: Solve from bottom to top.

Gauss-Jordan Elimination

Process: Reduce all the way to RREF.

Phase 1: Forward Elimination (reach REF).

Phase 2: Backward Elimination (reach RREF).

Algorithm: Partial Pivoting

  1. At each step, look at the current column.
  2. Find the row with the largest absolute value in that column (at or below current row).
  3. Swap that row to the pivot position.
  4. Why? This reduces round-off errors and ensures numerical stability in computer implementations.

Real-Life Application: Traffic Flow Network

A city intersection has flow rates satisfying conservation (In = Out):

  • Node A: \(x_1 + x_4 = 300\)
  • Node B: \(x_1 + x_2 = 250\)
  • Node C: \(x_2 + x_3 = 150\)
  • Node D: \(x_3 + x_4 = 200\)
RREF RESULT \[\left[ \begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 300 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -1 & -50 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 200 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right]\]

Column 4 has no pivot → \(x_4\) is a free variable. We can express the entire flow in terms of \(x_4\): \(x_1 = 300 - x_4, x_2 = x_4 - 50, x_3 = 200 - x_4\).

Real-Life Application: Chemical Balancing

Balance: \(aC_3H_8 + bO_2 \to cCO_2 + dH_2O\)

Conservation: Carbon (3a=c), Hydrogen (8a=2d), Oxygen (2b=2c+d).

Smallest integer solution via RREF: (1, 5, 3, 4).
Result: \(C_3H_8 + 5O_2 \to 3CO_2 + 4H_2O\)

Common Errors in Elimination

  • Arithmetic Mistakes: Adding when you should subtract, or missing a sign change.
  • Stopping Too Early: Thinking you are in REF when there are still nonzero entries below a pivot.
  • Forgetting Consistency: Trying to solve a system that has a row like \([0 \; 0 \; 0 \; | \; 5]\) (Contradiction).
Comprehensive Problem Set (5.10)

1. Use Gauss-Jordan to solve: \(x+y=6, 2x-y=3\).

Solution: RREF is Identity with [3, 3] constants. \(x=3, y=3\).

2. Identify an inconsistent system from its reduced matrix.

Solution: Any row of the form [0 0 ... 0 | k] where k ≠ 0.

Summary & Best Practices

Wrapping it all together

Common Errors to Avoid
  • ❌ Forgetting to check the solution in BOTH equations.
  • ❌ Using a dashed line for \(\leq\) or \(\geq\).
  • ❌ Shading the wrong half-plane (always test a point!).
  • ❌ Multiplying only one term in an equation instead of the entire row.
