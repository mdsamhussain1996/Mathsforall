Introduction & Overview

The foundation of simultaneous constraints

A system of linear equations is a set of two or more linear equations in the same variables. In most cases, we work with two equations and two variables (usually \(x\) and \(y\)). A solution to such a system is an ordered pair \((x, y)\) that satisfies every equation in the system simultaneously. Geometrically, this solution corresponds to the point where the lines represented by the equations intersect.

There are three possible outcomes when solving a system: the lines intersect at exactly one point, the lines are parallel (no solution), or the lines are identical (infinitely many solutions). Understanding these systems is crucial for solving real-life problems like break-even analysis, where you find the point where costs and revenue are equal, or resource allocation problems in engineering and economics.

📖 Core Vocabulary

  • System of linear equations: A set of two or more linear equations in the same variables.
  • Solution of a system: An ordered pair that is a solution of each equation in the system.
  • Consistent system: A system that has at least one solution.
  • Inconsistent system: A system that has no solution.
  • Dependent system: A consistent system with infinitely many solutions.

5.1 Solving Systems by Graphing

Visualizing the intersection of constraints

⚙️ Step-by-Step Method

1 Graph each equation in the same coordinate plane.
2 Estimate the point of intersection.
3 Check the point by substituting into both original equations.

Worked Example 1: Basic Graphing Solution

Solve the system:
\(y = -2x + 5\) (Equation 1)
\(y = 4x - 1\) (Equation 2)

Step 1: Graph both lines. Equation 1 has y-intercept 5 and slope -2. Equation 2 has y-intercept -1 and slope 4.

Step 2: The lines appear to intersect at (1, 3).

Step 3: Check:
Eq 1: \(3 = -2(1) + 5 \rightarrow 3 = 3\) ✓
Eq 2: \(3 = 4(1) - 1 \rightarrow 3 = 3\) ✓

Worked Example 2: Roofing Contractor (Real-Life)

A roofing contractor buys 30 bundles of shingles and 4 rolls of roofing paper for $1040. In a second purchase, the contractor buys 8 bundles of shingles for $256. Find the price of each.

System:
\(30x + 4y = 1040\)
\(8x = 256\)

From the second equation, \(x = 256/8 = 32\). Substituting into the first: \(30(32) + 4y = 1040 \rightarrow 960 + 4y = 1040 \rightarrow 4y = 80 \rightarrow y = 20\).

Interpretation: Price per bundle = $32, Price per roll = $20.

Practice Problems (5.1)

1. Check if (2, 6) is a solution for: \(x + y = 8\) and \(3x - y = 0\).

Solution: \(2+6=8\) (Yes) and \(3(2)-6=0\) (Yes). It is a solution.

2. Solve by graphing: \(y = -x + 7\) and \(y = x + 1\).

Solution: Intersection at (3, 4).

5.2 Solving Systems by Substitution

Algebraic precision through variable isolation

⚙️ Step-by-Step Method

1 Solve one equation for one variable.
2 Substitute that expression into the other equation and solve.
3 Substitute the found value back to get the second variable.
4 Check in BOTH original equations.

Worked Example 1: Direct Substitution

Solve:
\(y = -2x - 9\) (Eq 1)
\(6x - 5y = -19\) (Eq 2)

Step 1: Eq 1 is already solved for \(y\).

Step 2: Substitute \((-2x - 9)\) for \(y\) in Eq 2:
\(6x - 5(-2x - 9) = -19\)
\(6x + 10x + 45 = -19\)
\(16x = -64 \rightarrow x = -4\)

Step 3: \(y = -2(-4) - 9 = 8 - 9 = -1\).

Solution: (-4, -1).

💡
Pro Tip: Choose the equation where a variable already has a coefficient of 1 or -1. This minimizes fractions and makes the algebra much cleaner!
Practice Problems (5.2)

Solve using substitution: \(x + y = 180\) and \(x = 2y\).

Solution: \(2y + y = 180 \rightarrow 3y = 180 \rightarrow y = 60, x = 120\). Solution: (120, 60).

5.3 Solving Systems by Elimination

Removing variables through addition and subtraction

⚙️ Step-by-Step Method

1 Multiply one or both equations by a constant so coefficients of one variable are opposites.
2 Add the equations to eliminate that variable.
3 Solve the resulting single-variable equation.
4 Substitute back to find the other variable.

Worked Example 2: Multiply One Equation

Solve:
\(-10x + 3y = 1\)
\(-5x - 6y = 23\)

Step 1: Multiply Equation 2 by -2 to get \(10x\).
\(-2(-5x - 6y) = -2(23) \rightarrow 10x + 12y = -46\)

Step 2: Add to Equation 1:
\((-10x + 3y) + (10x + 12y) = 1 + (-46)\)
\(15y = -45 \rightarrow y = -3\)

Step 3: \(-10x + 3(-3) = 1 \rightarrow -10x - 9 = 1 \rightarrow -10x = 10 \rightarrow x = -1\).

Solution: (-1, -3).

Choosing the Best Method

Method When to Use
Graphing To estimate solutions or visualize relationships.
Substitution When a variable in either equation has a coefficient of 1 or -1.
Elimination When like terms have the same or opposite coefficients.
Elimination (Mult) When no variable can be directly eliminated.

5.4 Special Systems

No solution vs. Infinitely many solutions

Case 1: No Solution

Lines are parallel. They have the same slope but different y-intercepts. Algebraic solving leads to a contradiction (e.g., \(0 = 5\)).

Case 2: Infinitely Many Solutions

Lines are identical. They have the same slope and same y-intercept. Algebraic solving leads to an identity (e.g., \(0 = 0\)).

Worked Example 1: No Solution

Solve: \(y = 2x + 1\) and \(y = 2x - 5\)

By substitution: \(2x + 1 = 2x - 5 \rightarrow 1 = -5\). This is false. No solution.

5.5 Solving Equations by Graphing

Using systems to solve single-variable equations

To solve \(ax + b = cx + d\) graphically:

  1. Write \(y = ax + b\) (Eq 1)
  2. Write \(y = cx + d\) (Eq 2)
  3. The x-coordinate of the intersection point is the solution.

5.6 & 5.7 Linear Inequalities

Graphing regions and overlapping solutions

Graphing Steps
  1. Graph boundary line (Dashed for \(<\), \(>\); Solid for \(\leq\), \(\geq\)).
  2. Test a point (usually \((0,0)\)).
  3. Shade the side that makes the inequality true.
Systems of Inequalities

The solution is the overlapping region where all individual inequality shaded areas intersect.

Worked Example 4: Fruit Salad Budget

At most $10 on grapes ($2.50/lb) and apples ($1/lb).
Inequality: \(2.5x + y \leq 10\)

Test (0,0): \(2.5(0) + 0 \leq 10 \rightarrow 0 \leq 10\) ✓. Shade toward the origin. Solutions must be in Quadrant I (positive weights).

Summary & Best Practices

Wrapping it all together

Common Errors to Avoid
  • ❌ Forgetting to check the solution in BOTH equations.
  • ❌ Using a dashed line for \(\leq\) or \(\geq\).
  • ❌ Shading the wrong half-plane (always test a point!).
  • ❌ Multiplying only one term in an equation instead of the entire row.
