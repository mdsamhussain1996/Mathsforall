Kinematics

Forward and inverse kinematics — mapping joint angles to positions

Forward Kinematics

Given: Joint angles θ₁, θ₂, …, θₙ — Find: End-effector position and orientation. We chain together rotation matrices — each joint's frame relative to the previous one — to get the full transform.

T₀ⁿ = T₀¹ · T₁² · … · Tₙ₋₁ⁿ x = L₁cos(θ₁) + L₂cos(θ₁+θ₂) y = L₁sin(θ₁) + L₂sin(θ₁+θ₂)

Inverse Kinematics

Given: Desired end-effector position — Find: The joint angles. IK is generally much harder than FK — multiple solutions may exist (or none). Solved analytically for simple arms, or numerically using the Jacobian.

θ₂ = arccos((x²+y²−L₁²−L₂²)/(2L₁L₂)) θ₁ = atan2(y,x) − atan2(L₂sin(θ₂), L₁+L₂cos(θ₂))

Coordinate Transformations

Rotation matrices, homogeneous transforms, and quaternions

Rotation Matrices

A rotation matrix R maps vectors from one coordinate frame to another while preserving lengths and angles. In 2D, rotating by angle θ gives a 2×2 matrix. In 3D, we have rotations about x, y, and z axes that can be composed.

R(θ) = [[cos θ, -sin θ], [sin θ, cos θ]] (2D) Properties: Rᵀ = R⁻¹, det(R) = 1

Homogeneous Transforms

We combine rotation and translation into a single 4×4 matrix. This elegantly handles the full rigid-body motion: rotation + position. The DH convention uses these to describe robot kinematics systematically.

T = [[R | p], R: 3×3 rotation [0 | 1]] p: 3×1 position

Quaternions

A more compact and numerically stable representation of 3D rotations. Avoids gimbal lock, interpolates smoothly (SLERP), and is used in virtually all modern robotics and game engines.

q = w + xi + yj + zk, |q| = 1 v' = q v q* (rotation formula)

Differential Equations

ODEs, trajectory modeling, and motion equations

Why ODEs in Robotics?

Robot dynamics are governed by differential equations. Newton's second law F = ma, Euler's equations for rigid body rotation, and Lagrangian mechanics all lead to systems of ODEs that describe how a robot evolves in time.

M(q)q̈ + C(q,q̇)q̇ + g(q) = τ M: inertia matrix C: Coriolis/centripetal terms g: gravity vector τ: joint torques

Numerical Integration

Euler's method, Runge-Kutta 4th order (RK4) — these let us simulate continuous dynamics on a digital computer. Choose step size carefully: too large causes instability, too small wastes computation.

Euler: y_{n+1} = y_n + h·f(t_n, y_n) RK4: k₁ = f(t, y), k₂ = f(t+h/2, y+h·k₁/2)

Trajectory Planning

Given start and end states, find a smooth trajectory. Cubic or quintic polynomials in joint space, or splines in task space. Constraints: velocity = 0 at endpoints, within actuator limits.

  • Euler & Runge-Kutta integration
  • Lagrangian & Newtonian dynamics
